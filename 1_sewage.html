Is sewage actually liquid gold?

Read this (~4 min) — your scientist is right here, ask anything

Here's a strange fact about modern life: farmers spend billions of dollars a year buying nitrogen and phosphorus to put ON the land — while cities spend billions more removing the exact same chemicals FROM their water. We buy it, eat it, flush it, then pay to destroy it.

Most of the treasure is in urine. Pee is only about 1% of what flows into a wastewater plant, but it carries most of the nitrogen and roughly half the phosphorus. Your body alone sends about 4 kilograms of nitrogen down the toilet every year — a big bag of lawn fertilizer, per person, per year, thrown away.

Why does that matter? Making fresh nitrogen fertilizer from air (the "Haber-Bosch" process) burns enormous amounts of energy — one of the biggest industrial energy users on Earth. And phosphorus is mined from rock that won't last forever. Meanwhile the same elements stream under every city, every day, in the sewer.

So scientists — including the one at your table — build machines that pull the good stuff back out. One approach uses electricity and special membranes to grab ammonia (a nitrogen compound) straight out of wastewater and turn it into usable fertilizer. A Stanford-built prototype literally runs on sunlight. The nickname in the field? "Liquid gold."

The catch: these machines slowly clog and wear out, and nobody fully understands what's happening inside while they run. That's where your scientist comes in: using X-rays at SLAC to watch the machines work in real time, from the inside. See the problem as it happens, and you can fix it.

Wait, really? (steal these for your Wow Hunt)

  • Urine = ~1% of wastewater but carries most of its nitrogen.
  • Making fertilizer from air uses around 1-2% of ALL the world's energy.
  • A Stanford prototype harvests fertilizer from pee using sunlight.
  • Phosphorus comes from mined rock — and good reserves are limited.

Words you'll hear

Electrochemistry — using electricity to drive chemical reactions. Membrane — a super-thin filter that lets some molecules through and blocks others. Operando X-ray imaging — X-raying a device while it runs, like a live MRI for machines.
