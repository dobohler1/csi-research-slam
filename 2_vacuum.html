Why does SLAC's 2-mile machine have to be emptier than outer space?

Read this (~4 min) — written by your SLAC scientist. Ask them anything.

Did you know? The vacuum inside a modern particle accelerator is often better than the vacuum in outer space near Earth.

Imagine a race car in a snowstorm. Picture driving a Formula 1 car through a snowstorm. Every snowflake that hits the car slows it down and pushes it off course. Particle beams face the same challenge. Even though air looks empty, it's full of billions of molecules. When particles traveling near the speed of light slam into those molecules, the beam loses energy and quality.

What is a vacuum? A vacuum is a space with very few gas molecules. Scientists use pumps to remove almost all the air from the beam pipe, creating a clear path for electrons or protons to travel without interference.

Why is vacuum so important?

• Particles can travel kilometers without hitting a gas molecule.
• The beam stays focused and stable.
• Less radiation from unwanted beam losses.
• Sensitive equipment stays protected.
• Experiments give more accurate results.

How good is the vacuum? The air around you holds roughly 25,000,000,000,000,000,000 molecules per cubic centimeter. Inside some accelerator beam pipes, the pressure is more than a trillion times lower than the air's.

How empty?

Classroom air — very dense. Outer space near Earth — extremely thin. Accelerator vacuum — often even better than space.

Real examples. Scientists at SLAC's Linac Coherent Light Source — and at CERN's Large Hadron Collider — rely on ultra-high vacuum systems. Without vacuum technology, these machines couldn't accelerate particles or make powerful X-ray beams.

Careers in vacuum science. Vacuum scientists and engineers design the pumps, chambers, sensors, and cleaning steps that make discovery possible — for particle physics, fusion energy, computer-chip manufacturing, space exploration, and medicine.

Think like an engineer!

  • What would happen if someone opened a valve and let air into a running accelerator?
  • How long would it take to pump it back down to vacuum?
